Air Pollution

We understand that the Jeepney has always been a traditional transport system that has been used by Filipinos for decades, but such liabilities that affects our respiratory system as passengers and the drivers themselves are at risk due to its fossil fueled engines. To add to that, due to the Vast amount of Fossil Fueled engine jeepneys are in the Philippines creates an effect to the atmosphere of cities and roads as a whole. There are recent studies of jeepneys being a one of the major sources of Black Carbon (BC) in the Philippines, especially in Metro Manila. This is a residue of combustion processes which have created higher Ambient Emission levels that can be life threatening to Filipinos. This can be a significant health hazard for our drivers which could be fatal if left unattended. One could say that we have used jeepneys for years and it has not been a detrimental issue for us. But with population going higher and the problems of Air pollution and Climate change arising throughout the years along with the creation of new jeepneys, we should not wait till it becomes too much for us to handle. Jeepneys with Poor Emission, mostly especially around atleast 15 years old or more are more under the threat of being phased due to excessive emissions and harmful gases which can be very damaging to the commuters and the drivers themselves. Majority of the engines are usually Japanese diesel engines that have been restored, and their exhausts frequently emit black soot. Jeepneys have been found to be a significant contributor to air pollution, accounting for 48% of Manila's airborne particulate matter and more than 15% of the nation's road transportation greenhouse gas emissions.

Safety and Comfort

The (PUVMP) Public Utility Vehicles Modernization Program, would also want a safer drive for our passengers. With the help of Modernized jeeps, having a An anti-lock braking system (ABS) and adjusting the designs of cranky sounds, balding tires, unsteady suspenses of jeepneys to enhance the welfare of commuters and Drivers of Jeepney public transportation. According to the article Design of the Philippine Jeepney for Crashworthiness Analysis: A Finite Element Analysis Approach, using computer-aided design (CAD) and FEA models created in SIEMENS NX, the FEA Design Centre Facility of the Metals Industry Research and Development Centre (MIRDC) of the Department of Science and Technology was able to assess the vehicle crashworthiness. The results of the simulated impact analysis showed that the current jeepney design is not well suited to withstand such crash impacts. Consequently, it may result in fatal injuries that could endanger its passengers. With the number of jeepneys growing in numbers on the road, it is best to have newer and modernized ones for the safety of the people and the commuters. Jeepney Operators are very much trustworthy when it comes to managing and using PUVs which was proven throughout the years but this doesn't reassure us that the vehicle itself could 100% guarantee safety amongst its commuters and could possibly have more possible injuries especially regarding the older jeepneys that has not been properly modified thorughout the years which leads to higher chances of fatal injuries and crashes.

Counterarguments

1. Phasing out jeepneys would indeed affect the livelihood of a lot of people, but remember that with the transition will come job opportunities. Phasing out of jeepneys could lead to increased demand in jobs focused in manufacturing, driving and maintaining the new modern, eco-friendly vehicles. As per the plan of the government to subsidize the transition, they can offer programs to train drivers and mechanics alike affected by the transition. This also means that everything becomes more regulated and formalized, which indicates better wages and benefits for everyone in the sector.

2. Initially, the transition would be challenging, change is always demanding especially when it is conducted in an enormous scale. But something to consider is, as this is a government initiated project, the government then are incentivized to invest in the project. This means building the infrastructure needed to support electric vehicles, including giving subsidies for the manufacturers of EV's and builders of the charging stations. This support would accelerate the availability of both the modernized vehicles and its infrastructure. Additionally, this can attract private investors which could further accelerate the release of charging stations and production of the jeepneys. This can enable local production of modernized jeepneys, relying on already existing companies or entrusting the task to ones who would like to enter the industry.

3. Modernization might actually avoid fare increases or at the very least mean only a minimal increase with the promise of increased comfort and reliability for commuters. Modernized jeepneys, who run on electric engines, tend to be more energy-efficient and have lower maintenance costs than traditional models. These means increased savings for operators, which can offset the cost of modernization, helping to avoid the need to raise fares significantly. In fact, with lower fuel costs, fares might even decrease over time.

4. Cultural preservation would not require continuous operation. These relics can be put in a museum for everyone to view and reminisce about. The cultural significance of jeepneys lies primarily in their design, history and symbolism rather than it being a staple of public transportation. We have no need to keep them in the roads in order to preserve the culture.

Rebuttals

1. Job opportunities may not necessarily match the skills of existing jeepney drivers and operators. A lot of drivers are self-employed or rely on informal work, and transitioning to new roles, may require skills that they don't currently have. Aside from that, government-provided training programs are often limited and may take time to implement. Especially for people who rely on working and earning daily to feed their families, they may not be able to take time away from work to attend training.

2. These are all good concepts to talk about, but there is only uncertainty that comes with these. Government-initiated projects are reliant on political priorities, and any disruption could lead to delays and funding shortages. Additionally, building the infrastructure for electric vehicles is a massive project. In areas where the development is slow, jeepney drivers may struggle to access charging stations, disrupting their operations and impacting their income. Infrastructure should be built reliably first, to make the transition convenient and feasible for the drivers.

3. The initial puchase price for the modernized jeepneys are deermined to be significantly higher than traditional jeepneys. Even with lower fuel and maintenance costs, it will take time to recoup these upfront expenses, putting the drivers and operators under unnecessary debts. Even if modernization ultimately results in cost savings, there's no guarantee that saving from fuel efficiency and reduced maintenance will be reflected in fare prices, if anything the operators can choose to still pass the costs to the commuters if they see an opportunity to raise fares.

4. Jeepneys hold a unique cultural significance that cannot be captured or preserved fully by confining them to museums. They are part of a living culture, seeing them in action on the roads shows a cultural symbol that resonates with Filipinos' daily lives. A museum cannot capture the role jeepneys play as a unique form of public transportation.
