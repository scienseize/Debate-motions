Arguments

Negative effects of contraceptives

Contraceptives can have a negative effect on one’s body. Especially with hormonal contraceptives, as they can have adverse effects such as a disturbance to your sleep pattern, headaches, hair loss, as well as even cardiovascular diseases. Education focusing on how to have safe sex may gloss over the negative side effects of hormonal contraceptives in favour of teaching students how to have sex in a safe manner

Teaching responsibility and consent

Focusing on teaching students responsibility, consent, and pleasure is a more viable option towards sex education. These topics of interest can help bring guidance towards youth on what to do and make an informed decision on what to do in regards to sex and attraction within their relationships. It is far more important and impactful to a student to learn these than the simple, scientific knowledge behind contraception as they may not know what else to do with the information of contraceptive education without any guide around the other factors when having sexual relations, especially because young students do not have the necessary life experience and psychological intelligence.

Religious values

Religious communities often employ abstinence as their form of prevention, as well as a form of devotion to their religion. Mere focus on contraceptive education can sideline the equally valid option of abstinence, as well as disconnect sexual safety from religious values. A lack of inclusion towards the religious ideas about sex can undermine the religious values and morals they hold towards it. This is divisive for the reason that a large majority of people in the Philippines are religious. This means that contraceptive education shouldn’t be the primary focus of sex education within the Philippines and it does not have to be mandatory. Other aspects of sex education deserve the spotlight in order to educate the students for them to have a healthy relationship to their sexuality.

Counterarguments

Knowledge about contraceptives, while indeed reducing teen pregnancy, won’t necessarily decrease sexual risk behaviours in youth. Young people may still have an early sexual initiation in spite of knowledge about how to combat the health risks of having unprotected sex. A lack of knowledge on how to deal with sexuality and how to properly navigate relationships in regards to sex is also a factor on why teen pregnancy rates are so high.

Aside from teen pregnancy, early sexual initiation can also increase the likeliness of getting depression having suicidal thoughts. As I’ve explained, it can not only introduce several health risks to someone to have sex without prior knowledge, but also psychologically damage them.Thus, guiding someone to have responsibility towards their sexual health as a youth is more worth it than prioritising contraceptive education.

Contraceptives also (aside from contraception like tube ligation and vasectomies) are not 100% effective, and sometimes not affordable (such as with injected contraceptives and of course IUDS and the aforementioned methods of tubal ligation and vasectomy). Using sex ed as a way to promote a safe space to discuss topics about sex can open the door to discussions about natural planning and how to navigate their sex life if contraceptives don't work or are not an option.

Sex Ed that focuses on interpersonal relationships and the wellness of students can also destigmatize sex as something that isn’t dirty to talk about. Destigmatizing sex ed can create comfortability around the topic of sex and make it easier for students to ask for guidance around their sexual health and diminish any sense of shame they may have around it.

Rebuttals

Teen pregnancy can be deterred not only by contraceptives. Knowledge of the consequences of unprotected sex can indeed reduce teen pregnancy. However, so can teaching children about how to have sex in a manner that also protects their mental and emotional wellbeing. More goes into protecting youth from teen pregnancy than knowledge about contraceptive methods. Some youth may feel pressured into having unprotected sex even when they have access to contraception. Teaching about consent, learning how to choose your partners, what to do when you’re in a sexually charged situation but you’re not ready, as well as cultivating attitudes that value sexual honesty constitute sexual responsibility. These behaviours can reduce teen pregnancy through methods that aren’t just contraceptives.

While teen pregnancy is painful both on an emotional and physical scale, young students may not fully grasp the real, detrimental consequences that could occur if they choose not to use contraception.

Religious students could still have a sex educator that can provide guidance that adheres to their values while still teaching comprehensive sex education primarily focused on sexual responsibility. Also, while there are many religious students, that doesn’t mean that abstinence-only is the kind of sex education that should be taught. Of course, religious students also deserve to have a comprehensive sex education as they may have sex at one point or another. The reason religious views should be considered is that religion can have an impact on how one values and views sexual intimacy, and that should not be overshadowed and deserves to be given guidance for.
