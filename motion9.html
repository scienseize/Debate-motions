Terms and Definitions

Sustainable Forestry: seeks to satisfy human demands for wood and other forest products while preserving the health of the forest for future generations. It makes an effort to protect animals while also attending to the social requirements of nearby tribes and towns who rely on the forest

Agroforestry: the intentional integration of trees and shrubs into crop and animal farming systems to create environmental, economic, and social benefits.

Restoration Ecologist: works to repair an ecosystem or natural habitat that has been damaged by humans, especially through industrial processes.

Arguments

It's good for the environment

Planting trees must be a prerequisite for graduation. Planting a tree after graduation can make a positive impact for the environment in more ways than one. One of the most significant benefits trees offer in our environment is their role in combating climate change all across the globe. Trees are able to absorb carbon dioxide, a major greenhouse gas into their system, through photosynthesis. This is important as the mass amount of carbon dioxide around the world is increasing the temperature of our planet thus causing climate change. Climate change has become one of the single biggest health threats facing humanity as a whole. Climate change is already directly harming health through pollution, diseases, drastic weather events, forced displacement, and poor nutrition where people are not able to grow sufficient food. By planting trees, students can offset their own carbon footprint and contribute to global efforts to reduce the effects of climate change.

Trees also serve as protection by preventing soil erosion. The root systems of the trees anchor the soil place which reduces the risk of erosion in areas prone to heavy rainfall or on slopes which is especially prominent in the philippines. This helps maintain soil fertility which is critical for agriculture and prevents landslides from occurring. Additionally, trees serve as an important role in managing water resources. Their roots can absorb excess water, reducing surface runoff and helping to prevent flooding which is also prevalent in the philippines. Thus, this also helps replenish groundwater supplies, improving overall water quality and conservation efforts. Moreover, trees also support biodiversity by providing natural habitats for a wide range of wildlife, including birds, insects, and small mammals. When students plant trees, they can help create or restore ecosystems that offer shelter and food for these species which then contributes to the preservation of biodiversity. Planting trees after graduation contributes to a greener, more sustainable world. Planting trees should be a prerequisite for graduation as It is a gesture that can have lasting environmental benefits for the whole planet.

To conclude, planting a tree after graduation is a meaningful way for students to leave a lasting legacy. It symbolizes personal growth, environmental responsibility, and commitment to the future. Through the years, the tree will only continue to grow, inspire others, and provide long-term benefits to the planet as a whole thus becoming a living testament to the student’s positive impact on the world.

Reduces air pollution/Gives more oxygen

Planting trees after graduation must be implemented as it can play a significant role in reducing air pollution in both immediate and long-term ways. One of the primary benefits trees offer is their ability to absorb carbon dioxide through the use of photosynthesis. Trees take carbon dioxide from the air and convert it into oxygen which is then released back into the atmosphere. By planting trees, students directly contribute to reducing concentrations of carbon dioxide helping to control its harmful effects on the environment and reduce the overall greenhouse effect. Additionally, trees are effective at filtering out other harmful pollutants from the air. These pollutants include sulfur dioxide, ammonia, nitrogen oxides, and particulate matter, all of these are common byproducts of industrial activities, vehicle emissions, and power generation. Trees act as natural air filters, absorbing these harmful substances into their leaves, bark, and roots. This process helps improve air quality, making the environment healthier for people, animals and plant life in the surrounding area.

Furthermore, trees can improve air circulation in urban environments. When trees are planted in clusters or along green corridors, trees help to create airflow that disperses pollutants, preventing them from accumulating in one area– the natural ventilation helps lower the concentration of harmful air substances, therefore improving overall air quality. By encouraging students to plant trees before graduation, they will be promoting a cleaner and fresher air in neighborhoods and cities, especially those with high levels of industrial activity and traffic pollution like Makati.

In conclusion, planting a tree after graduation is a simple but effective way for students to help reduce air pollution. Through absorbing carbon dioxide and other pollutants, cooling urban environments, and providing long-term environmental benefits, trees play a critical role in improving air quality. By planting a tree, students not only contribute to cleaner air, but also inspire others to take action, creating a lasting legacy of environmental care and responsibility.

Counterarguments

1. While planting trees is good for the environment, simply planting a tree does not guarantee it will survive or contribute meaningfully to the ecosystem. Trees require consistent care, especially in their early stages, to ensure survival and growth. Many students may not have the resources, knowledge, or ability to provide ongoing care, which may result in high mortality rates for the planted trees. Poorly managed tree-planting intitiatives can lead to wasted resources without lasting benefits. A study published in Environmental Science and Pollution Research emphasizes the importance of enhancing and securing permanence of carbon stored in trees and soils, noting that poorly managed tree planting could negatively impact global warming efforts.

Better leave it to nature

This ambitious project is too much and can overwhelm the lands and the trees that grow themselves in this amount. The apparent is that forestry has always depended on planting trees. However, some restoration ecologists have criticized this strategy, claiming that planting and caring for young trees is costly and wasteful of limited resources. They also point out that if plantations are cut and used to make short-lived wood products like paper or cardboard, the carbon stored in growing trees is swiftly released into the sky.

In few well-researched case studies, planting trees has even had unfavorable results. For example, the expansion of forest cover in China's Loess Plateau led to increased soil erosion and decreased water availability for agriculture and human use. Subsidies for tree planting in Chile produced a twisted incentive to plant trees rather than preserve natural forests. There was no net change in the amount of carbon stored in trees nationwide during the 2006–2011 period, and the strategy caused a loss of natural forest cover.

There is ample proof that spontaneous regeneration has been successful in accelerating the restoration of biodiversity and forest biomass. Natural regeneration is sometimes seen as a solution that benefits both the environment and economic growth.

The results of a 20-year study that attempted to answer this topic were just published. A multinational team evaluated the amount of carbon contained in the remaining trees in a Malaysian tropical forest that had been cleared for logging in the 1980s and 1990s. After that, the amount of carbon monitored retained over a 20-year period in both naturally regenerating areas and nearby patches that had been extensively restored by planting trees and removing climbers and other invasive plants. Comparing the two, it was discovered that the forest that was being actively restored was storing carbon 50% more quickly than the forest that was being allowed to naturally regenerate. Without the need of human interference, forests were able to properly adjust and regenerate themselves on their own. Much more effective and no need for us to intervene. No cost at all, just keep it safe growing on its own.
