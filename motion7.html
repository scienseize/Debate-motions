Government Side

Terms and Definitions

Promote: means to actively support, encourage, or advance the growth, awareness, acceptance, or use of a product, service, idea, or cause.

E-vehicles: are vehicles powered by electric motors instead of internal combustion engines that use fossil fuels like gasoline or diesel. EVs operate by drawing electricity from a rechargeable battery pack, which supplies energy to the motor to propel the vehicle.

Arguments

Cleaner Environment

The ability of electric vehicles to lower greenhouse gas emissions, including carbon dioxide (CO2), methane (CH4), and nitrous oxide (N2O), is one of its most remarkable environmental benefits. Traditional internal combustion engines (ICEs) are replaced by electric motors in electric vehicles. Plug-in hybrid electric cars (PHEVs) and battery electric vehicles (BEVs) fall under this category. PHEVs utilize a combination of battery and ICE power, while BEVs use electricity produced by an electric battery. Since it is an electric vehicle, it does not have a tailpipe and all the usual parts for liquid fuel, such as a fuel tank, fuel line, or fuel pump. Zero tailpipe emissions from electric cars mean that dangerous pollutants, including particulate matter, nitrogen oxides, and volatile organic compounds (VOCs), are not released into the atmosphere. Because they use little to no fossil fuels, have fewer moving parts that require maintenance, and have low running costs (petrol or diesel), electric vehicles are very environmentally friendly. One of the biggest problems in the Philippines is the exhibition of high pollution, and because of this, it is encouraged to make efforts to change the number of vehicles that are more environmentally friendly. Reducing harmful exhaust emissions is good for our health. Improved air quality will lead to fewer health problems and reduced expenses related to air pollution. Additionally, EVs produce less noise pollution than cars powered by gasoline and diesel since they are quieter.

Economic Savings

Compared to fueling a car with gasoline or diesel, charging an electric car is generally more economical. The financial certainty of driving an EV is increased by the more constant electricity prices. Compared to ICE vehicles, electric vehicles have a lot fewer moving components, which results in significantly lower maintenance costs and requirements. To encourage the use of electric vehicles, numerous governments around the world provide tax breaks, rebates, and financial incentives. A nation's policies to fulfill its commitment to declared sustainability goals include these incentives. The Philippine government has made electric jeepneys, or e-jeepneys, mandatory vehicles since they have become a more environmentally friendly option than traditional diesel jeepneys. Air pollution in the Manila metropolitan area and other urban centers is the main objective of the Public Utility Vehicle Modernization Program (PUVMP), which seeks to replace internal combustion engine (ICE) jeepneys with environmentally friendly and energy-efficient electric models. To make this transition, funding has been provided to cooperatives that provide jeepney services. A Comprehensive Roadmap for the Electric Vehicle Industry (CREVI) was introduced by the government with the introduction of the EVIDA law. It offers significant benefits for manufacturing, R&D, human resource development, and components for EVs and charging stations. This change should save working companies, drivers, and passengers a lot of money, especially when combined with greatly reduced operational costs. More than PHP 1,000 would be saved daily for each jeepney unit if energy costs alone were reduced from PHP 1,500–1,800 for diesel to only PHP 500–700 for electricity.

Technological Advancement

Improved vehicle control system capabilities make our roads safer, and the drive for EV innovation has resulted in smarter transportation networks. These technologies also help to improve urban mobility and reduce congestion in traffic. Our quality of life is improved by technological advancements in many ways. From smarter home appliances to improved communication systems, they increase the efficiency and convenience of daily tasks. Innovation across a range of industries produces safer and more dependable products, such as better medical equipment and advanced transportation systems. Improvements in materials science and manufacturing techniques help other sectors by producing more sustainable and effective goods. Because of EV technology's knock-on effects, smart grid systems have developed more quickly, improving the dependability and environmental friendliness of our energy infrastructure.

Opposition Side

Terms and Definitions

Promote: means to actively support, encourage, or advance the growth, awareness, acceptance, or use of a product, service, idea, or cause.

E-vehicles: are vehicles powered by electric motors instead of internal combustion engines that use fossil fuels like gasoline or diesel. EVs operate by drawing electricity from a rechargeable battery pack, which supplies energy to the motor to propel the vehicle.

Arguments

High Acquisition and Maintenance Costs

The cost of EVs in the Philippines remains prohibitive for many consumers. There is a substantial price difference between EVs and their regular cars counterparts. The fact is that many cannot afford to invest in EVs. Despite the zero-tariff rates on EV imports until 2028, the price difference still remains unappealing to many people especially the new working class who is trying to acquire their first cars through bank financing options offered by car dealerships. Also the zero-tariff policy applies only on certain EV imports, many models still incur taxes and fees that drive up the prices. These costs make EVs substantially more expensive for Filipino consumers, especially when compared to gasoline-powered vehicles, which are better established and has lower prices. As most EVs are imported from different countries. Take Nissan as an example, the baseline price for the Nissan Leaf, the staple electric vehicle of nissan, is PHP2M, while Nissan Almera, a gasoline-powered sedan car with the same look as the Leaf costs only PHP850K. This huge of a difference is a dealbreaker for most households.

The battery for electric vehicles is one of the most expensive parts of an EV. As an example, for the Nissan Leaf, a full replacement including labor costs, and other fees and taxes, may cost an owner of up to PHP500K. Without local manufacturing and recycling facilities, the costs may become even higher depending on the availability of the replacement parts.

EV challenges

Charging Stations Availability

The limited charging infrastructure for EVs in the Philippines presents a significant barrier to widespread adoption. It restricts the practicality and convenience of owning an EV. As of 2024, there are only around 300 charging stations in the country, and a huge concentration of these stations are located in urban centers like Metro Manila. This scarcity means that many areas, particularly rural and remote locations, have little to no access to charging facilities, making EV ownership and travel challenging outside major cities. This lack of infrastructure is a huge concern, as a driver, I can only imagine the constant worry about running out of battery power without access to a nearby charger. This problem is compounded by the fact that most of the current stations offer slow to moderate charging speeds, which makes the vehicle less ready for increased usage.

Expanding the EV charging network would require substantial investment and planning, which poses additional challenges. Unlike other countries, Philippines has limited public funding and private investment directed toward EV infrastructure, slowing the pace of the development. Additionally, installing fast-charging stations is expensive and requires a stable power supply, which the country's current electrical grid may not reliably support, especially in the provinces. Without a widespread, reliable charging network, a promotion of e-vehicles would seem more like a sham aimed to get investments from more users. As there is a lack of convenient charging options, this limits the EV usage to mostly city-bound trips, undermining the potential environmental and economic benefits of shifting to electric vehicles. sources:

The non-renewable energy problem

E-vehicles in the Philippines rely heavily on the country's existing power grid, which remains largely dependent on non-renewable energy sources like coal, oil and natural gas. As of 2022, 59.6% of the total power generation output is attributed to coal, while natural gas contributing 16%. This dependency on fossil fuel limits the environmental benefits of EVs, since charging them with electricity generated from non-renewable sources still contributes to the greenhouse gas emissions.

Promoting E-vehicles without increasing renewable energy production could strain the power grid and affect the electricity output in the country. Developing renewable energy capacity involves significant investment and long-term planning, which we have not done yet. Until the country shifts toward a more renewable energy-based grid, the environmental gains from EVs will be off-set, as they remain indirectly tied to the non-renewable energy powering them

Counterarguments

1. Despite the high upfront cost of EVs, they offer substantial savings on fuel and maintenance over the vehicle's lifetime. Unlike gasoline-powered cars, EVs are more efficient and have fewer moving parts, which reduces the likelihood of mechanical issues. EV owners can save significantly on fuel costs as electricity is often cheaper than gasoline. This long term savings can offset the initial purchase price.

Additionally, as EV technology continues to advance and more models enter the market, EV prices are expected to decline. With the recent entrance of Tesla to the Philippine market, we see the price of their cheapest model marked at PHP2.1M. Also to be noted, the popular electric car, Nissan Leaf, is PHP800k cheaper now than it is 3 years ago. With further time, more price cuts can be expected.

2. Possible counter revolves around recognizing the lack of infrastructure in existence. They might say something about how infrastructure does not need to be fully developed at the start. And that with the adoption of EV and the growth of demand, so too will the demand for charging stations, naturally incentivizing both public and private sectors to invest in charging infrastructure.

All the costs does not have to be shouldered by the government. Charging infrastructure development can be achieved through public-private partnerships, reducing the financial burden. Private companies have already started exploring EV charging stations as an additional service to customers. Shell, SM Supermalls, and Ayala Malls have all taken initiative in setting up EV charging stations. With more partnerships like these, charging stations can become commonplace without relying solely on government resources. In Singapore, charging stations are mainly concentrated in high-demand areas, with plans to expand based on usage patterns and data gathered. The Philippines could adopt a similar approach by building in key areas like Metro Manila, Metro Cebu and Davao.

3. With regards to non-renewable energy, it is possible to say that even if the electricity used to charge EVs comes from non-renewable sources, EVs still by itself contribute immensely to emissions reductions due to their higher energy efficiency. Electric motors convert more energy from the power grid into motion that internal combustion engines convert gasoline, meaning that EVs still emit less CO2. Studies have shown that, even on grids powered by fossil fuels, EVs generally produce fewer lifecycle emissions than gasoline vehicles. This means that transition to EVs can reduce emissions in the short term, while the grid is gradually decarbonized.

Rebuttals

1. Regardless of the comparison in the form of long-term savings, the high upfront cost of EVs still remain a significant financial barrier for majority of Filipino consumers. With the average household income in the Philippines being relatively low, even the cheapest EVs on the market are still out of reach for most people. The long-term savings on fuel and maintenance are only appealing and beneficial to those who can afford paying the high initial investment, making it inaccessible to most of the population. As long as the cost for EVs is double of a gasoline-powered car, there is no world where it is accessible to the average Filipino household. In a country where many consumers rely on second-hand vehicles due to affordability, expecting people to invest in even a "low-cost" EV priced at PHP2M is unrealistic. The initial cost will deter a large portion of the population, regardless of potential future savings.

Additionally, while electricity is generally cheaper than gasoline, fluctuating energy prices and increasing demand may result into a different story. The Philippines has one of the highest electricity prices in Southeast Asia, and as more EVs enter the market, the demand for electricity could drive prices up. In 2022, electricity rates in the Philippines spiked, impacting household budgets. As EV adoption increases, a similar spike in demand could increase electricity prices, which would diminish the cost savings associated with EV ownership.

While EVs have fewer mechanical parts, the cost of repairing or replacing its high-tech components, such as batteries and software systems, can be more expensive. In the Philippines, EV maintenance facilities and technicians are limited, as well as supply of parts, which could drive up repair costs.

Philippine Star reports that the country's grid frequently experiences power interruptions, particularly during peak demand seasons, which poses a challenge for reliable EV charging.

The current approach which focuses on high-demand urban areas, leaves rural areas without adequate access. This is a sign of failure for a nationwide EV adoption. Private companies have little incentive to install chargers in places with limited EV ownership. 3. Although EVs are more efficient than traditional combustion engines, charging them on a coal-dependent grid, like the one we mainly use in the Philippines, results in substantial emssions. With the current grid mix, of 60% coal power, it means that the environmental benefits of EVs are compromised. As long as the power grid is largely non-renewable, switching to EVs doesn't drastically reduce emissions. A study from the Union of Concerned Scientists highlights that EV emissions are heavily influenced by the energy mix used to power them. In Philippines with a high coal dependency, EVs can produce more greenhouse gases than hybrid vehicles.

This means, hybrid vehicles may offer a more immediate solution. Hybrid vehicles produce less emissions than their EV counterparts on regions with high dependency on coal. Hybrids can operate with lower emissions compared to full EVs when charged on a fossil-fuel heavy grid, as they combine a smaller electric battery with a combustion engine. The National Renewable Energy Laboratory research indicates that hybrids may produce fewer emissions in regions where grid electricity is largely generated from coal. Hybrids are also more affordable for many consumers, providing a practical solution until renewable infrastructure is in place.
