Arguments

Rice is a staple food

Statista states that Filipinos have consumed about 16 million metric tons of rice in the marketing year of 2022/2023. However, the USDA Foreign Agricultural Service has stated that the Philippines has only produced 12.63 million metric tons of rice in 2022/2023. Philrice also supports this. This is troubling because the population of the Philippines will only increase as the years pass. As stated before, the production of rice in the span of one year has been significantly less than the rate of consumption for that same year. As the population grows, the demand for rice will grow in tandem. However, with the local production of rice falling behind by such a significant amount, it alone will not be able to satisfy the needs of the Filipino people. With local production not being able to satisfy demand compared to supply, we must rely on importation in order to feed our people.

Lack of fertile land

Some reasons why the Philippines cannot satisfy the demand of rice via the means of local production is due to the fact we do not have enough fertile land to cultivate crops due to a lack of river deltas, unlike Vietnam, which has an advantageous geography (The Red River Delta and Mekong Delta) to produce plenty of crops. Another reason is that we are falling behind on agricultural technology. Compared to Vietnam, who have employed various means to increase the cost-effectiveness of their rice production, the Philippines have been left behind in terms of mechanization and modernization.

Subject to natural disasters

The third and damndest reason as to why we must allow the importation of rice into the Philippines is that we are very often subject to unpredictable weather phenomena and natural disasters that can spell calamity for our farms. These can range from typhoons, to floods, as well as droughts. These phenomena, exacerbated by global warming, cause our farms to flood, or dry up, and simply ultimately become destroyed and unusable with crops wasted. Since we cannot control the weather, our agricultural efforts will continue to be negatively affected by these weather conditions. The significant destruction and loss of crops are an incentive for us to import rice from other countries, as if we were to deny importation, it would be impossible for us to make up the difference with the remaining crops that we have. Such reasons are why the importation of rice is something we require in the Philippines. Merely relying on our own means of production would only cause us to fall into hunger as crop yields would be insufficient for our large population’s dependence on rice.

Counterarguments

There are different varieties of genetically modified rice such as Sahod Ulan (which is more resistant to droughts) and Submarino 1 (which is more resistant to flooding) to name a few. These rice varieties can combat the destructive weather conditions within the Philippines. However, we still lose several crops in spite of these resilient rice varieties existing. This is because rice farmers use higher yielding varieties, and other flood-resistant varieties that are being cultivated right now have not been tested in real farms where they can experience the real, harsh conditions of nature. Since rice yields are still insufficient even though we have modified varieties, it is not enough to fulfill the needs of every filipino.

We also must spend on rice as, like what was said before, local rice production isn’t enough to fill the bellies of the Filipino people. Our local rice yield has only grown from 8.7Mil metric tons to 12.9Mil metric tons from 2002 to 2022. Meanwhile, our rice consumption has increased from 8 Mil metric tons to 15~16 Mil metric tons from 2002 to 2022. Our rice consumption has grown nearly twice the amount in over 20 years and our local rice yield has been unable to keep up. Even if we invest into our agriculture to make our rice farms more self-sufficient, we still won’t be able to keep up in a long time, especially because of the growing population. Thus we need importation, even if we put our efforts towards self-sufficiency, because self-sufficiency won't catch up to the rate at which we consume rice.

Rebuttals

Creating new rice varieties can take years of experimenting. The Sub1 gene, which is responsible for letting rice survive underwater for 2 weeks, was discovered in 1991. The IR64 Sub1 (a.k.a Submarino 1), which contains the gene, was released throughout the Philippines 18 years later, in 2009. While creating new strains of rice that can survive the weather conditions of the Philippines would be good for our country’s self-sustainability, it would take several years. Years in which we do not have enough local rice to satisfy our people. With climate change getting worse as well, (a predicted temp increase of ~2C and a rainfall increase in Luzon and Visayas by 2050) weather patterns will change with storms becoming more destructive and droughts to increase, according to the UN. Weather changes could affect the effectiveness of our crops’ genetic modifications. With the Philippines’ geographical location making us more vulnerable to these destructive weather patterns as well as the cultivation of resistant rice strains taking years, the Philippines’ local rice production is likely to suffer from shortages in the coming years. This is also coupled with the fact that we still suffer rice shortages even with the strains we currently use.

While there are rice terraces that have been abandoned and can/have been re-cultivated in order to help with our agricultural efforts, there’s a gap within our local rice production that can’t be filled within a short time. According to the USDA Foreign Agricultural Service, Vietnam’s rice yield is over twice ours. While our local rice production is still not enough, importation can fill the gaps in our supply.

Aside from importation being a short-term solution to our current inadequate supply, it should still be allowed even if we are able to be self-sustained as we can fall victim to the adverse weather and phenomena in our country at any point. Importation can also serve the purpose of allowing a larger amount of different kinds of rice for people to choose. Rice importation can serve to fill the gaps in local rice shortages, loss in rice crops and damage to rice agriculture, as well as being able to make different rice grains more accessible. Overall, rice importation is still important even when self-sufficiency becomes a reality for the Philippines.
